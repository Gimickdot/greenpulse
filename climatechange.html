Climate Change

_____________________________________________________________________

In recent decades, the term "climate change" has been predominantly used to describe the rapid and primarily human-induced alterations in the Earth's climate system,
especially the rise in global average temperatures and its associated impacts on ecosystems, weather patterns, sea levels, and human societies.
These changes are primarily attributed to the increased emission of greenhouse gases, such as carbon dioxide and methane,
resulting from activities like burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial processes.




Climate Change: How
Nature's Affected?



Terrestrial Ecosystems

Terrestrial ecosystems, encompassing forests, grasslands, deserts, and other land-based habitats, play a crucial role in climate change as they both respond to and influence climatic conditions, with alterations in temperature, precipitation, and carbon cycling affecting their biodiversity, productivity, and resilience.




Altered Phenology

Altered phenology in climate change refers to shifts in the timing of seasonal events, such as flowering, migration, and hibernation, among plants and animals, which occur as a response to changing climatic conditions, potentially disrupting ecological interactions and ecosystem functions.




Weather Events

Weather events in climate change refer to short-term atmospheric phenomena such as storms, heatwaves, droughts, and heavy precipitation, which are influenced by long-term shifts in climatic patterns and are increasingly characterized by greater frequency, intensity, and unpredictability due to anthropogenic climate change.




Biodiversity Loss

Biodiversity loss in climate change refers to the decline in the variety and abundance of plant and animal species, driven by the impacts of changing climatic conditions such as habitat destruction, altered temperature and precipitation patterns, and the disruption of ecological relationships, posing significant threats to ecosystems and their resilience.
